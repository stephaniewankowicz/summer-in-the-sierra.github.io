This service is exactly that. I'll tell you what I'd do if I were going on your trip — without the vague language and generic advice.

Approach

How I think about Sierra planning

1

Permits first, route second

Most people plan the trip they want and then try to fit a permit to it. That's backwards. Build your plan around what permits are actually achievable for your dates and group size, then optimize the route within that zone. A great itinerary with an unobtainable permit is just a document.

2

Current conditions matter more than guidebooks

The Sierra is a high-snowpack range. Creek crossings that are knee-deep in July can be dangerous in June. Passes that feel routine in August might be snow-covered in early season. I factor in the current year's snowpack and conditions in every plan — not just historical averages.

3

Honest mileage, not optimistic mileage

A 15-mile day at altitude with 3,000 feet of gain is not the same as a 15-mile day on the coast. Plans that ignore this leave people wrecked on day two. Every itinerary I write is built around what's actually sustainable for the group at elevation.

4

Bailouts aren't a failure case — they're part of the plan

Weather, injury, and unexpected conditions happen. Every Custom Itinerary and Full Planning package includes explicit bailout routes at key decision points, so you know your options before you're standing in the rain at 11,000 feet trying to read a map.
